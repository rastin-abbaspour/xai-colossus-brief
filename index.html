The energy and emissions footprint of xAI's Colossus data centers

In roughly four months, xAI assembled one of the world's largest AI training clusters inside a former factory in South Memphis. The local grid could not deliver the power it needed, so the company ran dozens of natural gas turbines on the property. This brief traces what that decision meant for electricity demand, air pollution, water, and public accountability.

Section 1 · The problem

A machine that outran its power grid

xAI assembled its Colossus supercomputer in South Memphis in roughly 122 days. The site had just 8 megawatts of grid power at first, against a machine that would soon draw hundreds. The utility caught up slowly, and the gap in between was met by burning gas on site.

Section 2 · The build

Dozens of turbines, few permits

Trucked-in natural gas turbines multiplied on the site. By spring 2025, surveys counted about 35 turbines running, roughly 420 MW, comparable to a mid-size power plant, most of it operating before any air permit was issued. After legal pressure, xAI kept 15 permitted units in Memphis, then moved 27 more across the state line to Southaven, Mississippi.

Section 3 · The emissions

Likely the largest industrial NOx source in the region

Nitrogen oxides are the headline pollutant. Using manufacturer emission factors, advocates estimate the Colossus 1 turbines could emit 1,200 to 2,000 tons of NOx a year, more than any single established industrial source nearby, including the airport, a chemical plant, and an oil refinery.

Section 4 · Beyond nitrogen oxides

Particulates, carbon monoxide, formaldehyde, and carbon

The Southaven turbines carry a wider pollution profile on a potential-to-emit basis. On climate, xAI's own filing for a proposed permanent 41-turbine plant projects a substantial greenhouse-gas total.

Section 5 · The water

Cooling that draws on a drinking-water aquifer

The heat that drives the power demand has to be carried away, and Colossus cools with water drawn from the Memphis Sand aquifer. Design projections were large, but metered use has so far been far lower. A recycling plant meant to cut aquifer reliance broke ground in October 2025, then stalled.

Metered peak of about 381,000 gallons per day (August to September 2025) sits well below the multi-million-gallon design figures. The $80M, 13-million-gallon-per-day recycling plant is projected to save about 5 billion gallons of aquifer water a year once built.

Section 6 · The accountability gap

Build first, permit later, no fine

Governance lagged far behind construction. Permits arrived after the turbines were already running, an appeal was dismissed on procedural grounds, and by mid-2026 no regulator or court had levied a single monetary penalty, even as the federal government stepped in to defend continued operation.

  • June 2024
    Colossus 1 goes online; turbines begin running without air permits.
  • April 2025
    Aerial and thermal surveys document about 35 turbines, most actively running.
  • June 17, 2025
    SELC, for the NAACP, files a 60-day notice of intent to sue under the Clean Air Act.
  • July 2, 2025
    Shelby County grants a permit for 15 turbines, after more than 1,700 public comments.
  • Dec 16, 2025
    The local air board dismisses the permit appeal, citing mootness.
  • April 14, 2026
    The NAACP sues xAI and its subsidiary MZX Tech in federal court over 27 unpermitted Southaven turbines.
  • Mid-2026
    The U.S. Department of Justice moves to dismiss, arguing Grok supports national security. Still no fine levied.

Why it matters for UNU INWEH. Memphis compresses the resource and governance tensions of the AI boom into two sites and two years. When grid interconnection lags demand, operators substitute on-site combustion, turning a computing decision into a local air, climate, and water liability, borne first by an overburdened community.
